Access reviews are designed for compliance.
They're not designed for reviewers.

Haritha Reddy · June 2026 · 5 min read

Every access review process I've seen looks clean on paper. Send the review. Reviewer validates access. Approve or revoke. Capture evidence. Close the campaign. The compliance box gets checked. But the actual decision quality — whether access that shouldn't exist gets removed — is a different question entirely.

After owning an IAM platform covering 50,000+ users and 200+ enterprise applications, the pattern I kept seeing wasn't malicious rubber-stamping. It was something more mundane and harder to fix: reviewers approving access they didn't fully understand, because the alternative — revoking access without enough context — felt more risky than leaving it in place.

The approval bias problem

When a reviewer opens an access certification and sees a list of role names, group memberships, and entitlement strings — many of which are technical identifiers that only make sense to the IAM team or the engineering team that built them — their options are limited.

They can approve everything, which takes two minutes and carries a low perceived risk of causing an incident. They can revoke something, which takes judgment, context they may not have, and carries a real risk of breaking something for a colleague who then escalates to their manager. Or they can ask someone in IAM or engineering what an entitlement actually does — which takes time they don't have during a quarterly review window.

Approval is not the path of least resistance because reviewers are lazy. It's the path of least resistance because the process gives them insufficient context to confidently do anything else.

The result is access certifications that generate compliance evidence without meaningfully reducing access risk. The campaign completed. The evidence exists. The rubber stamp problem remains.

Why this is a product problem, not a people problem

It's tempting to frame this as a training problem or an accountability problem. If reviewers understood the entitlements better, or if there were consequences for rubber-stamping, the quality would improve.

That framing misses the structural issue. At enterprise scale — thousands of reviewers, hundreds of thousands of access relationships, quarterly cadences — you cannot train your way to good decision quality. The reviewers are often managers or application owners with full-time jobs that are not access governance. Expecting them to become part-time IAM analysts every quarter is not a sustainable model.

The product question is: how do we design the review experience so that a non-specialist reviewer can make a confident, accurate decision in the time they realistically have?

That's a different problem than "how do we enforce that they review carefully." Enforcement without usability just increases friction and resentment. It doesn't improve decision quality.

What reviewers actually need

From watching how access reviews get done in practice, the context that most changes reviewer behavior falls into a few categories:

  • Is this access being used? Usage data is the single most actionable signal. An entitlement that hasn't been exercised in 90 days is a much easier revocation decision than one with no usage information at all.
  • Is this access sensitive? A reviewer making a decision about access to a general productivity tool should be making a different kind of decision than one reviewing access to financial systems or customer data. Sensitivity context changes the appropriate level of scrutiny.
  • Is this normal for this role? Peer comparison — "3% of people with this job title have this access" — gives reviewers a baseline. Outlier access is easier to question when the outlier nature is visible.
  • What changed since the last review? Static lists of all access are harder to process than delta views that highlight what's new or changed since the previous certification.
  • What breaks if we remove it? Dependency context — even a simple "this access is required for application X" — dramatically reduces the perceived risk of revocation decisions.

None of this information is exotic. Most mature IAM platforms have access to most of it. The gap is in surfacing it at the moment of the review decision, in a format that a non-specialist reviewer can act on quickly.

The goal is a confident decision, not just a completed review

This is the reframe that I think matters most for anyone building or improving access review tooling: the outcome we're optimising for is not campaign completion rate. It's not even revocation rate, which can be gamed by revoking low-risk access that nobody uses anyway.

The outcome is confident, accurate decisions — reviewers who can look at an access item, understand what it is, understand the risk of leaving it in place versus removing it, and make a call they'd stand behind if asked to explain it.

A completed review that produces no real access reduction is compliance theater. The goal is not the evidence. The goal is the outcome the evidence is supposed to represent.

Getting there requires treating the reviewer as the user — designing the review experience around their constraints, their context, and their actual decision-making process — rather than designing it around the audit requirement and expecting reviewers to adapt to it.

Where AI changes this

The context problem in access reviews is exactly where AI-assisted governance has real near-term value. Not AI making revocation decisions autonomously — that's the wrong place to remove the human — but AI synthesising the usage data, peer comparison, sensitivity signals, and dependency context into a recommendation that a reviewer can accept, modify, or override with one click.

The reviewer stays accountable for the decision. The AI does the work of assembling the context they need to make it well. That's a meaningful improvement over the current state, where the context exists somewhere in the system but never reaches the reviewer at the moment they need it.

Access reviews won't be fixed by making them more frequent, more mandatory, or more consequential to ignore. They'll be fixed by making it easier to make a good decision than to make a fast one. That's a product problem. And it has a product solution.

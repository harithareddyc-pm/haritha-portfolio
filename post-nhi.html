NHI · Governance

We're governing the credential.
We should be governing the behavior.

Haritha Reddy · June 2026 · 5 min read

Non-human identities — service accounts, bots, API tokens, workloads, scripts, integrations — are one of the fastest-growing identity populations in any enterprise. And one of the least well-governed.

The reason isn't that teams don't care. It's that we're applying the wrong model. Most NHI governance programs are built on the same foundation as human identity governance — the Joiner-Mover-Leaver (JML) framework. And JML is the wrong lens for non-human identities, because non-human identities don't behave like people.

The JML problem

When a person joins an organisation, we provision access. When they move teams, we adjust it. When they leave, we deprovision. The lifecycle has clear trigger points, clear owners, and clear outcomes.

Non-human identities don't have any of that.

A service account doesn't "join" — it gets created for a purpose, often by an app team under time pressure, often with broader access than strictly needed because scoping it precisely would take another week. It doesn't "move teams" — it just keeps running, often long after the team that created it has been reorganised or the original developer has left. And it doesn't "leave" in any clean sense — it accumulates dependencies, becomes load-bearing for some integration nobody fully mapped, and suddenly deprovisioning it feels riskier than leaving it alone.

Service accounts don't have a lifecycle event. They have a creation event — and then they just run.

This is how you end up with service accounts that are years old, with standing privileged access, no documented owner, no expiry date, and no clear answer to the question: does this still need to exist?

The ownership gap

The hardest part of NHI governance isn't technical. It's ownership.

In most enterprises, the responsibility is genuinely fragmented. IAM teams define the controls. App or platform teams create the accounts. Security teams worry about the risk. Compliance teams want the evidence. But when you ask "who actually owns this identity and what it does?" — the honest answer is often: nobody, specifically.

IAM thinks it's an app team problem because they created the account. The app team thinks it's an IAM problem because they don't manage credentials. Security is tracking the risk without the context to resolve it. And the account keeps running.

This fragmentation isn't a gap in policy — it's a gap in product design. The governance tooling and processes weren't built with non-human identities as a primary use case. They were retrofitted. And retrofitting human-identity governance onto machine identities produces exactly the outcomes you'd expect: incomplete coverage, unclear accountability, and risk that compounds quietly over time.

What actually needs to change

The shift I think matters most is moving from governing the credential to governing the behavior behind it.

Credential governance — rotating secrets, vaulting passwords, enforcing expiry — is necessary. But it's not sufficient. A rotated credential on an over-privileged service account with no documented owner is still a risk. You've secured the key. You haven't secured what the key opens.

Governing behavior means being able to answer five questions for every non-human identity in scope:

  • Why does this identity exist? What purpose was it created for, and is that purpose still active?
  • What can it do? What systems, data, and actions does its current access permit?
  • Who owns it? Not who created it — who is accountable for its access and actions today?
  • Is it still needed? Is it actively being used, or has it gone dormant while retaining its access?
  • What would happen if it were misused? What's the blast radius if this identity is compromised or acts outside its intended scope?

Most organisations can answer the first two for maybe half their non-human identity population. The last three are where the real exposure lives.

Why this matters more now

The NHI governance problem isn't new — but it's becoming more urgent for two reasons.

First, the population is growing faster than governance can keep up. Cloud-native architectures, microservices, CI/CD pipelines, and infrastructure-as-code mean that machine identities are being created at a rate that manual processes can't track. The service account that used to be an exception is now the norm.

Second, AI agents are arriving as a new category of non-human identity — one that's more autonomous, more capable of cross-system action, and less predictable in its behavior than a traditional service account. The governance gaps that exist for machine identities today will be significantly more consequential when the identity in question can make decisions, call APIs, and take actions across your enterprise without a human in the loop.

Getting NHI governance right now is the prerequisite for governing AI agents safely later.

The teams that figure out behavioral governance for service accounts and API tokens in the next two years will be significantly better positioned to govern AI agent access than the teams that don't. The problems are related. The discipline translates.

The product framing

For product managers working in this space: NHI governance is fundamentally a product problem, not just a process or policy problem. You can write all the policies you want about service account ownership and access review cadence. If the tooling doesn't make it easy to answer those five questions, the policies won't be followed consistently at scale.

The right product question is: how do we make it easier for the teams who create non-human identities to govern them well — rather than making governance something that happens to them from the outside, in a process they don't fully understand or own?

That's the shift that actually changes outcomes. Not tighter controls on top of the same broken ownership model — but a governance model that's designed for how non-human identities actually work, not for how human identities used to work.

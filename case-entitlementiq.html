EntitlementIQ — Identity Risk Dashboard

A concept exploration of how access governance teams could prioritize entitlement reviews using live risk signals — rather than fixed-schedule audits that treat every account equally.

Type
Concept exploration
Domain
IGA · Access governance · NHI
Status
Design concept

The problem

Access governance teams typically review entitlements on a fixed schedule — quarterly certifications, periodic audits — regardless of whether an account's risk profile has changed. A dormant service account and a user account with a recent spike in unusual access requests get the same review cadence, even though they represent very different levels of risk.

The question this concept addresses: if every identity had a live risk rating, what would a reviewer's workflow actually look like — and how would that signal change what they prioritize?

The core idea

Risk shouldn't be a separate report you check before a review — it should be part of the review workflow itself. A HIGH risk badge on a dormant service account should surface that account at the top of the queue, not just add a color to a table. The concept keeps risk visible at every step: dashboard, review queue, and individual account view — so reviewers triage by risk, not by alphabetical order or last-modified date.

This connects directly to NHI/NUA governance: non-human identities (service accounts, API tokens, machine credentials) are exactly where risk signals matter most and receive the least continuous attention. Nobody's "using" a service account day to day in a way that surfaces anomalies the way a human login pattern would — so a risk rating becomes the proxy for the attention that would otherwise be missing.

Key design decisions

Risk-first queue
Reviews ordered by risk, not schedule
HIGH risk accounts surface first in the review queue. Reviewers don't need to scan everything — risk score does the triage for them.
Core
Signal transparency
Score is explainable, not a black box
The risk rating is composed of underlying signals (dormancy, entitlement breadth, anomalous activity) — reviewers can see why an account is HIGH, not just that it is.
Critical
NHI focus
Service accounts are first-class citizens
Non-human identities appear alongside human accounts in the dashboard — not in a separate tool that gets reviewed quarterly and ignored otherwise.

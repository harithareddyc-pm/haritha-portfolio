AI Agents · Identity

AI agents aren't apps.
IAM needs a third identity class.

Haritha Reddy · June 2026 · 6 min read

The default response when an AI agent needs enterprise access is to give it a service account. It's the closest familiar category. It has an identity, it has credentials, it can be scoped to specific systems. The box gets checked. The agent gets access. The governance question gets deferred.

I think this is the wrong model — and the longer enterprises run with it, the harder it will be to correct when something goes wrong.

Why agents are different

A traditional service account represents a specific, bounded function. It authenticates, it performs a defined set of operations, and its behavior is largely deterministic. The same API call in, the same result out. Governing it means governing the credential and the permissions attached to it.

An AI agent is fundamentally different because it acts based on context, not a fixed script. A single agent might read from an inventory system, query a customer database, call a vendor API, write a summary to a document, and trigger a workflow — all in the course of completing one task. Its behavior varies based on what it encounters. Its scope at runtime is determined by what the task requires, not what was anticipated at provisioning time.

The governance question for a service account is: what does this account have access to? The governance question for an AI agent is: what is this agent allowed to decide, and who gave it that authority?

Those are genuinely different questions. They require different governance models.

The two existing models and why they don't fit

Human identity governance

Access request → approval workflow → role assignment → periodic recertification. Designed around stable roles and predictable access patterns. Works because humans have managers, job titles, and defined responsibilities that map to access needs.

Service identity governance

Credential provisioning → secret rotation → access scoping → expiry enforcement. Designed around bounded, deterministic functions. Works because service behavior is predictable and the blast radius of a misconfiguration is relatively contained.

AI agent identity — the gap

Agents need elements of both and something neither provides: runtime policy enforcement based on what the agent is trying to do in the moment, not just what it was provisioned to do at setup time.

What's missing

Session-level revocation when behavior drifts. Scoped, time-limited access grants per task. Human escalation for sensitive actions. Audit trails that capture decisions and context, not just API calls.

The questions that need answers

When I think about what AI agent governance needs to address, it comes down to six questions that neither existing model handles well:

  • Who gave the agent authority? Not who provisioned the credential — who approved the agent to act on behalf of a user, a team, or a business process, and under what constraints?
  • What is it allowed to do? Not just what systems it can access, but what categories of action it's permitted to take — read, write, trigger workflows, make decisions, delegate to other agents.
  • What should require human approval? There's a category of actions that an agent should be able to propose but not execute autonomously — write access to financial systems, actions that affect customer data, anything with significant or irreversible consequences.
  • How do we limit scope at runtime? The access granted at provisioning time should be a ceiling, not a floor. What the agent actually uses for a specific task should be the minimum required for that task — issued temporarily, scoped narrowly, expiring when the task completes.
  • How do we trace what happened? Not just the API calls, but the decisions: what did the agent reason about, what did it choose, what did it escalate, what did it skip?
  • How do we stop it quickly? If an agent starts behaving outside expected boundaries — unusual data volume, lateral movement across systems, actions inconsistent with its stated task — how fast can we kill the session, and does that require a human in the loop or can it be automated?

The gate between intent and action

The concept I keep coming back to is a runtime control point — a gate that sits between the agent's intent and the action it wants to take. Not a provisioning-time gate that grants broad standing access and hopes for the best, but a per-action or per-task gate that evaluates each request against policy in real time.

This gate does three things that existing controls don't:

  • It issues access for the specific task, not the broadest anticipated use case
  • It routes high-risk actions to a human reviewer before they execute, not after
  • It watches the active session for behavioral drift and revokes access if the agent acts outside what was approved

The goal isn't to slow agents down. The goal is to make agent access safe, explainable, and governable — which is actually a prerequisite for deploying them at scale in sensitive enterprise environments. The teams that are nervous about AI agents in production are nervous precisely because they can't answer the six questions above. A runtime control point is what makes those questions answerable.

Where identity goes from here

The arrival of AI agents as an identity class isn't just a new governance challenge. It's a signal that identity governance is expanding beyond account management into something more fundamental — governing actions, delegation, context, and authority.

Human identity governance asks: does this person have the right access? Service identity governance asks: does this credential have the right permissions? Agent identity governance will need to ask: does this agent have the right authority, for this task, under these conditions, with these constraints?

Identity is becoming less about who you are and more about what you're allowed to decide. That's a bigger shift than it sounds.

For IAM product managers, this is the most interesting space in the discipline right now — not because it's well-defined, but because it isn't. The governance models don't exist yet. The tooling is early. The enterprises deploying AI agents are figuring out the access question in real time, often without a clear framework.

The teams that build that framework — that figure out what it means to govern an agent's authority rather than just its credentials — will be ahead of where the rest of the industry lands in two or three years. And the product managers who can articulate what that framework needs to look like are exactly the people those teams need.
